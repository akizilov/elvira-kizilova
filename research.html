Elvira Kizilova
Teaching Research About Me

My primary research interests are consumer well-being, emotions, and perceptions. This work covers multiple domains and contexts including consumer interactions with service environments (e.g., clinical, retail, and tourism) and related emotions that affect consumer well- being. I am interested in conducting research projects that have significant managerial and public policy implications and positive impact on consumer well-being. I use lab and field experiments, survey research, and quantitative methods of analysis. I am very interested in expanding my knowledge of content analysis. I believe that analysis of large Internet datasets, including social media data and corporate published materials, makes a strong contribution to the field of marketing.

My research work to date can be broken into three streams. The first studies access to health services from the consumers’ perspective, examining subjective (vs. objective) consumers’ perception of access. Through scale development and a series of three studies, the initial paper demonstrates that perceived access impacts perceived vulnerability, health-related behaviors and attitudes (such as distrust in a health care system and health motivation), and consumers’ overall health. We also extend these theoretical findings to various contexts, including the COVID-19 pandemic. Our second paper explores the role of perceived access to health care and pandemic fear in preventative COVID-19 behaviors and maintaining overall health and mediating effects of consumer vulnerability and health system concerns. Our third paper examines how U.S. abortion laws influence consumers’ subjective perceptions of access to health care and their overall health and well-being.

My second research stream explores how consumers process nutritional information presented with different formats of front-of-package (FOP) labels under different purchasing conditions. In this paper, we use two types of FOP labels: symbol-based (evaluative) and text- based (objective). In a series of lab and field studies, we test which consumers identify the healthiest product more easily, when they process label information directly vs. indirectly, and how their choices differ depending on the presence (vs. absence) of diet-related diseases. We are currently collecting online data for our second study.

My third research stream examines the role of anthropomorphic cues in promoting sustainable hedonic products and services. Specifically, we study the effectiveness of sustainable communications in tourism. Drawing on relationship marketing theory, we propose that including anthropomorphic cues and positive emotional framing in sustainability communications will increase positive consumer behavioral intentions through the perception of goal congruence and brand connectedness. This research contributes to the literature on sustainable communications and anthropomorphism and has clear managerial implications. We are currently designing and pretesting experimental stimuli for our second study.

In the future, I will continue to work in the areas of consumer emotions and perceptions that affect well-being, including extending sustainability labeling research to various cultures. Travel destinations are primarily promote themselves internationally, and sustainable attitudes and behaviors, as well as legal and societal norms and practices, vary across different cultures.

Additionally, I will explore positive and negative effects of anthropomorphism on consumer well-being. Most research has focused on anthropomorphism’s positive consequences for businesses. However, I would like to explore the consequences of anthropomorphism for consumers, including whether it induces consumer happiness and does not impose psychological threats.

I would also like to further explore consumer vulnerability in the war and post-war contexts. For example, the current war in Ukraine made many people vulnerable, whether they had to flee from their homes to other countries or stayed at home and had to survive Russian attacks. Some people became internally displaced twice – in 2014, when Russia occupied Crimea and parts of the Luhansk and Donetsk regions, and now in 2022. I would like to study how internally displaced people and refugees cope with their vulnerability. Other research streams may focus on war-and post war consumption of cosmetic products and beauty services and other types of hedonic consumption.
